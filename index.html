The Relative Clause — Direct, Indirect & Trigger Words
❧ ❧ ❧
An Clásal Coibhneasta Díreach
The Direct Relative Clause — used when the antecedent (head noun) is the subject or direct object of the relative verb
What is a relative clause? It qualifies a noun, like an adjective — giving more information about it.
an páiste a bhuaigh duais — the child who won a prize
an páiste a chónaíonn sa Rúis — the child who lives in Russia

The noun being described (páiste) is called the réamhtheachtaí (antecedent). The relative clause follows it and begins with a mír choibhneasta (relative particle).

Present & Future Tense — Struchtúr

Clásal Díreach — Aimsir Láithreach / Fháistineach
réamhtheachtaí + a + séimhiú + briathar The particle a causes lenition (séimhiú) on the following verb. It plays the role of who/which/that in English.
·an duine a fheicimthe person that I see (antecedent = object)
·an duine a fheiceann méthe person that sees me (antecedent = subject)
·an gasúr a fhaigheann na páipéir gach maidinthe boy who gets the papers every morning
·Cén uair a thiocfaidh sí inniu?When will she come today?
Special case — tá: tá does not lenite after a; instead they merge: bia atá réidh — food that is ready

Past Tense — Struchtúr

Clásal Díreach — Aimsir Chaite
réamhtheachtaí + a + séimhiú + briathar Structurally identical to the present — no visible difference, since regular verbs already lenite in the past tense anyway.
·an tuismitheoir a mhol na páistíthe parent who praised the children (or: whom the children praised)
·na ceoltóirí a bhain an chéad duaisthe musicians who won first prize
·Sin iad na mic léinn a rinne an obair.Those are the students who did the work.
NB — Vowels & f: Verbs beginning with a vowel or f in the past keep d'/d'fh: an béile a d'ullmhaigh tú — the meal you prepared

NB — Irregular verbs: Irregulars that don't lenite in the past (e.g. fuair) don't lenite in the relative clause either: an peata a fuair an tseanbhean — the pet the old woman got. Use the independent form of irregular verbs in the direct relative.

Ulster Irish — Foirmeacha Coibhneasta

Ulster & Connacht — Special Relative Endings
Láithreach: -(e)ann → -(e)as or -(e)anns
Fáistineach: -f(a)idh → -f(e)as
These special endings are used only in positive direct relative clauses in Ulster and Connacht. Not used in negative clauses or the past tense.
·an duine a mholasthe person who praises / that I praise
·an duine a mholannsthe person who praises (Ulster variant)
·an múinteoir a fheicfeas túthe teacher you will see
·Bheasis the relative form of beidh (will be)

Diúltach — Negative Forms

Negative Direct Relative Clause
Láithreach: réamhtheachtaí + nach + urú + briathar
Chaite (rialta): réamhtheachtaí + nár + séimhiú + briathar
Chaite (mírialta): réamhtheachtaí + nach + urú + briathar
·an fhuaim nach gcluinimthe sound I don't hear
·an bia nach n-ithimthe food I don't eat
·an mac léinn nár cheannaigh na leabhairthe student who didn't buy the books
·an scannán nach bhfaca méthe film I didn't see (irregular)
·Sin í m'aintín nach bhfeicim rómhinic.That's my aunt I don't see very often.

An Chopail — Copula in Direct Relative Clauses

Foirmeacha na Copaile — Clásal Díreach
The copula form does not change in direct relative clauses — use is / ba / nach / nár(bh) as normal.
Exception: ba → ab before a vowel or fh.
·an ceol is maith liomthe music I like (present)
·an carr ba mhaith liomthe car I would like (conditional)
·an rud ab fhearr liomthe thing I would prefer (conditional + vowel)
·rud nach ceartsomething that isn't right
·Sin an guthán póca ba mhaith liom do mo bhreithlá.That's the phone I'd like for my birthday.
An Clásal Coibhneasta Indíreach
The Indirect Relative Clause — used when the antecedent is linked to the verb through a possessive adjective, a preposition, or as an object pronoun
Key principle: The indirect relative clause is used in three situations:
1. The antecedent has a genitive/possessive relationship (= whose) — Feidhm Ghinideach
2. The antecedent is linked via a preposition (= whom I spoke to, the house I lived in) — Feidhm Thabharthach
3. The antecedent is the object with a resumptive pronoun — Feidhm Chuspóireach

1 · Feidhm Ghinideach — Possessive / "Whose"

Feidhm Ghinideach — Present & Future
réamhtheachtaí + a + urú + briathar + aidiacht shealbhach + ainmfhocal eile The possessive adjective (a, a, a, ár, bhur, a) agrees with the antecedent in number and gender.
·Sin í an bhean a bhfuil a mac san Astráil.That's the woman whose son is in Australia.
·Sin í an bhean a mbeidh a mac ag filleadh ón Astráil.That's the woman whose son will be returning from Australia.
·Sin an fear a raibh a mhac san otharlann.That's the man whose son was in hospital.
Feidhm Ghinideach — Past Tense (Regular verbs)
réamhtheachtaí + ar + séimhiú + briathar + aidiacht shealbhach + ainmfhocal
·Sin iad na daoine ar bhuaigh a bpáiste an chéad duais.Those are the people whose child won first prize.
·Sin é an fear ar cheannaigh a mhac gluaisrothar.That's the man whose son bought a motorbike.
Feidhm Ghinideach — Past Tense (Irregular verbs)
réamhtheachtaí + a + urú + briathar + aidiacht shealbhach + ainmfhocal Irregular verbs in the past use a + urú instead of ar + séimhiú.
·An tú an bhean a bhfuair a madadh bás?Are you the woman whose dog died?
·Sin é an fear a ndeachaigh a iníon chun na hollscoile.That's the man whose daughter went to university.
·Sin é an fear a ndeachaigh a cháil ar fud na tíre.That's the man whose reputation spread throughout the country.

2 · Feidhm Thabharthach — Prepositional Link

Used when the antecedent is linked to the clause through a preposition — e.g. the house I live in, the woman I stay with. A prepositional pronoun (forainm réamhfhoclach) appears at the end of the clause, agreeing in person, number and gender with the antecedent.
Feidhm Thabharthach — Present & Future
réamhtheachtaí + a + urú + briathar + forainm réamhfhoclach
·Sin é an teach a bhfanaim ann.That's the house I stay in. (ann = in it)
·Sin í an bhean a bhfanann siad léi.That's the woman they stay with.
·Sin iad na páistí a mbeidh tú ag tabhairt an leabhar dóibh.Those are the children you'll be giving the book to.
·Chonaic mé an siopa a mbíonn Micheál ag obair ann.I saw the shop Micheál works in.
·Sin í an bhean a bhfuil mo dheirfiúr ina cónaí léi.That's the woman my sister lives with.
Irregular verbs: Use the dependent form if one exists. E.g.: Cé hí an bhean a bhfaighfidh an duais uaim? — Who is the woman who will get the prize from me?
Feidhm Thabharthach — Past Tense (Regular)
réamhtheachtaí + ar + séimhiú + briathar + forainm réamhfhoclach
·Sin é an teach ar fhan mé ann.That's the house I stayed in.
·Sin í an bhean ar fhan siad léi.That's the woman they stayed with.
·Seo an dóigh ar chuala mé faoi.This is how I heard about it.
Feidhm Thabharthach — Past Tense (Irregular)
réamhtheachtaí + a + urú + briathar + forainm réamhfhoclach
·Sin é an teach a raibh siad ina gcónaí ann.That's the house they were living in.
·Sin é an carr a bhfaca mé ann tú.That's the car I saw you in.
·Ar chuala tú an scéal a raibh gach duine ag caint faoi ar maidin?Did you hear the story everyone was talking about this morning?

3 · Feidhm Chuspóireach — Object with Resumptive Pronoun

Used when the antecedent is an object and a resumptive pronoun echoes it inside the clause. Corresponds to English that/which/whom used as object in the middle of a sentence.
Feidhm Chuspóireach — Structure & Examples
Tá buidéal sa ghairdín. Thóg páiste é. →
Tá buidéal sa ghairdín ar thóg páiste é. The resumptive pronoun (é/í/iad) echoes the antecedent at the end of the clause.
·Tá leabhair sa leabharlann sin a n-osclaíonn daoine go minic iad.There are books in that library that people open often.
·Sin an tsióg nach bhfeicfidh duine ar bith í.That's the fairy that nobody will see.
·Sin í an bhean a bhfaca mé a carr taobh amuigh sa chlós.That's the woman whose car I saw outside in the yard.

An Chopail sa Chlásal Indíreach

Copula in the Indirect Relative Clause
The copula form in the indirect relative is ar (not is/ba as in the direct).
·an duine ar leis an carrthe person who owns the car (= to whom the car belongs)
·an cailín ar mhaith liom labhairt léithe girl I'd like to speak with

Diúltach Indíreach — Negative Indirect

Negative Indirect Relative Clause
Láithreach/Fáistineach: nach + urú + ... + forainm réamhfhoclach
Chaite (rialta): nár + séimhiú + ... + forainm réamhfhoclach
Chaite (mírialta): nach + urú + ... + forainm réamhfhoclach
·Sin é an teach nach bhfanaim ann.That's the house I don't stay in.
·Sin é an teach nár fhan mé ann.That's the house I didn't stay in.
·Sin é an teach nach raibh siad ina gcónaí ann.That's the house they weren't living in.
·Is bean í nach gcreidim focal uaithi.She's a woman I don't believe a word from.
Míreanna Ceiste & Coibhneasta
Interrogative and complementiser words that trigger either the direct or indirect relative clause
Key rule: Certain question words and connective words are followed by either the direct or indirect relative. Knowing which type a word triggers is essential for correct verb forms, mutations, and particles.

Díreach — Words that take the Direct Relative

MírCiall (Meaning)Sampla
cad / céard what Cad a tharla? / Céard atá ar siúl agat?
What happened? / What are you up to?
cathain / cén uair / cá huair when Cathain a fuair tú an carr nua?
When did you get the new car?
cá fhad how long Cá fhad a bhí sibh ann?
How long were you there?
cá mhéad / cé mhéad how many / how much Cá mhéad leabhar atá agat?
How many books do you have?
cé who Cé a bhí leat?
Who was with you?
conas / cad é mar how Conas a dhéanann tú é? / Cad é mar a chuir tú aithne air?
How do you do it? / How did you come to know him?
nuair when (conjunction) Cuirfidh mé scairt ort nuair a bheidh deis agam.
I'll call you when I get a chance.
chomh ... is/agus as ... as; however Coimeádfar iad chomh fada agus a bheidh siad de dhíth.
They will be kept as long as they are needed.
cé acu / cibé acu which one (of two); whether Is cuma liomsa cé acu a rinne tú é nó nach ndearna.
I don't care whether you did it or not.

Indíreach — Words that take the Indirect Relative

MírCiall (Meaning)Sampla
an áit / cá háit / cén áit the place / where Sin an áit a raibh sé. / Cá háit a mbíonn na ranganna ar siúl?
That's where he was. / Where are the classes held?
an chaoi / cén chaoi the way / how Cén chaoi ar tharla a leithéid?
How did such a thing happen?
an dóigh / cén dóigh the way / manner Seo an dóigh a n-ullmhaíonn sí a cuid pleananna ceachta.
This is how she prepares her lesson plans.
an fáth / cén fáth the reason / why Is é sin an fáth a raibh áthas orm. / Cén fáth a gcuireann tú do chuid ama amú?
That's why I was glad. / Why do you waste your time?
cad chuige why / what for Cad chuige a ndearna tú sin?
Why did you do that?
cad ina thaobh why / for what reason Cad ina thaobh ar inis tú bréag?
Why did you tell a lie?
an tslí / cén tslí the way Ní maith liom an tslí a bhfuil sé á chur i bhfeidhm.
I don't like the way it's being implemented.
a (= an méid a) what / as much as (amount) Ólann sé a dtuilleann sé. / D'ól sé ar thuill sé.
He drinks what he earns. (pres.) / He drank what he earned. (past)
gach a / gach ar everything that Faigheann sí gach a bhfuil ag teastáil. / Fuair sí gach ar theastaigh uaithi.
She gets everything that's needed. / She got everything she needed.
mar (= áit) where (place) Chuaigh mé go dtí an siopa éadaí mar a ndíoltar geansaithe áille olla.
I went to the clothes shop where lovely wool jumpers are sold.
Ulster note — cad é: In Ulster Irish, cad is typically heard as cad é (< cad é). This can precede the direct relative: Cad é a tharla? · Cad é atá agat?

ar vs a + urú in the past: The shift between ar + séimhiú (regular past) and a + urú (irregular past/tabharthach function) is the single most important thing to practise in the indirect clause.
